1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

This is an interactive 3D lesson that teaches business managers what a manifold
is, building from the simplest examples (curves, spheres) to charts, atlases,
homeomorphism, the torus, and the Klein bottle. The lesson prepares managers for
a later lesson on manifolds in machine learning.

The converged design uses:
- A "Museum Gallery" architecture (full-screen 3D exhibits with gallery wall
  pauses between acts)
- Failure-first pedagogy (show what ISN'T a manifold before what IS)
- Discovery-before-naming (every concept is experienced before labeled)
- Interactive verification at every stage (managers prove understanding by doing)
- A cartography/mapping metaphor as the unifying thread
- React Three Fiber / Three.js for 3D rendering

Total duration: ~55 minutes (Complete path) or ~35 minutes (Essential path).
Five mathematical concepts taught: Manifold, Chart, Atlas, Homeomorphism,
Non-orientable.

================================================================================
2. LESSON PHILOSOPHY & CORE PRINCIPLES
================================================================================

FAILURE-FIRST PEDAGOGY
The most important pedagogical decision. Every major concept is motivated by a
FAILURE that makes the concept feel necessary:
- Manifold: The figure-eight crossing fails the "locally flat" test
- Chart: One flat map can't cover the whole sphere (stalls at ~92%)
- Atlas: Coverage requires multiple overlapping maps
- Homeomorphism: Morphing sphere into torus tears (different genus)
- Non-orientability: The traced line returns reversed on the Klein bottle

DISCOVERY BEFORE NAMING
No technical term is ever introduced before the manager has experienced the
concept through interaction. The pattern is always:
1. Manager does something / sees something
2. Manager understands the concept intuitively
3. THEN the term is given a name
Example: Manager clicks points on a sphere, sees flat neighborhoods everywhere,
THEN hears "this is called a manifold."

VERIFICATION IS GENUINE
The manager must DO something — predict, classify, place, build — not just
click "next." The Five Comprehension Checkpoints (Section 17) are load-bearing
walls; if a manager can't pass one, subsequent acts will collapse.

NO MORE THAN 2 NEW TERMS PER ACT
- Act 1: "manifold"
- Act 2: "chart" and "atlas"
- Act 3: "homeomorphic"
- Act 4: "non-orientable"
The word "topology" is mentioned verbally but gets no definition card.

THE CARTOGRAPHY THREAD
The unifying metaphor throughout is mapmaking. Charts are map pages. Atlases are
map books. This isn't just analogy — it's the actual historical origin of the
mathematical terminology (Bernhard Riemann).

EXTRINSIC VIEW FIRST
In this lesson, the manager is the "mapmaker looking at shapes from outside."
The bridge to ML plants the seed: "Next time, you'll be ON the manifold, trying
to figure out its shape from the inside. That's what your data is doing."

4. OVERALL ARCHITECTURE: THE MUSEUM GALLERY
================================================================================

The lesson is structured as a walk through a museum gallery:
- Each act is a "room" with interactive 3D exhibits
- Between rooms: "gallery walls" — full-screen text/image cards that frame the
  next exhibit and provide breathing room
- Full-screen, single viewport (no persistent panels or split views)
- Split view introduced only in Act 2 when charts demand it, and in Act 4 for
  the dual-view torus/Klein bottle construction

TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE:
- Single persistent WebGL canvas (React Three Fiber)
- Scene content swapped between acts (no mount/unmount of R3F instances)
- Cross-fade transitions between acts:
  - Current exhibit dims (opacity -> 0.3 over 300ms)
  - Gallery wall fades in (opacity 0 -> 1 over 500ms)
  - Manager clicks Continue
  - Gallery wall fades out, new exhibit fades in
  - Total transition: ~1 second

AESTHETIC: Museum Gallery
- Background: deep navy (#0f172a)
- Text: Syne Bold for headings, DM Sans for body
- Color palette: sky blue (#38bdf8) for shapes, amber (#fbbf24) for interactive
  elements, green (#34d399) for "passes," pink (#f472b6) for "fails/tears"

6. COLD OPEN (30 seconds)
================================================================================

WHAT THE MANAGER SEES:
A coffee cup and a torus (doughnut), side by side, continuously morphing into
each other in a seamless loop. The morph is smooth — no tearing, no cutting.
The shapes flow between forms while maintaining their single hole.

NARRATION (text overlay, appearing line by line):
  "A coffee cup and a doughnut are the same shape."
  "Today you'll discover how — and why it matters for your data."

DESIGN SPECS:
- Both shapes rendered in sky blue (#38bdf8), semi-transparent
- Wireframe overlay at low opacity for depth
- Morph uses vertex interpolation on registered meshes (same (u,v) grid,
  80x80 vertices = 6,400 vertices, 12,800 triangles)
- Property Tracker badge in corner: "Holes: 1" — stays fixed throughout morph
- Auto-orbit at 0.3 rad/s, stops when manager interacts
- No jargon in the first 30 seconds (the word "topology" does NOT appear here)

WHAT IT MUST NOT DO:
- Front-load any technical vocabulary
- Require any interaction (this is a spectacle that earns attention)
- Show a percentage or loading indicator

7. ACT 1: LOCALLY FLAT — THE MANIFOLD PROPERTY (7-8 min)
================================================================================

Beat 1.1 — Figure-Eight Exploration (2 min)
--------------------------------------------
The manager sees a figure-eight curve (a lemniscate) on screen.

Challenge card: "This curve has a secret. Click different points and zoom in.
Can you find what makes one point fundamentally different?"

INTERACTION — Smart Zoom:
- Manager clicks points on the curve
- Camera smoothly dollies to 1.5 units from the clicked point (500ms, easeInOutCubic)
- Small context indicator (80x80px, top-left) shows full shape with glowing dot
- Click-to-click: camera pans directly without zooming out first (400ms)

WHEN MANAGER CLICKS SMOOTH POINTS:
- Inset panel (220x220px, top-right, #1e293b background) shows magnified local
  segment — a gentle arc, nearly straight
- Green text below: "Looks like a line ✓"
- After 3 smooth-point clicks, pattern is established

WHEN MANAGER CLICKS THE CROSSING POINT:
- Inset shows an X — two line segments crossing
- Pink text: "NOT a line ✗"
- Challenge card updates: "Found it! Two branches crossing — this point is
  fundamentally different."

ERROR HANDLING:
- No crossing found after 45 sec: crossing region glows amber (#fbbf24) at
  opacity 0.2, pulsing at 1Hz
- Still not found after 65 sec: text hint: "Look where two parts of the curve
  cross..."

WHAT THE MANAGER UNDERSTANDS:
"Most points on this curve look like a straight line when zoomed in. But the
crossing point doesn't — it looks like an X. Something is different here."

Beat 1.2 — Sphere Exploration (1.5 min)
-----------------------------------------
Figure-eight fades (400ms). Sphere appears (IcosahedronGeometry, 4 subdivisions,
radius 1.5, sky blue, opacity 0.7).

Challenge card: "Now try a surface. Click anywhere on this sphere and zoom in.
Can you find a point that ISN'T flat?"

FOR EACH CLICK:
- Tangent plane grid (8x8 amber lines, geodesic disc radius 0.3) appears at
  the clicked point
- Grid is nearly square everywhere — every point passes
- Green text: "Looks flat ✓"
- After 3-4 clicks, manager realizes: every point passes

PUNCHLINE (narration):
"Every small neighborhood on this sphere looks like a flat plane. There's no
'broken' point. Shapes like this — where every point has a flat neighborhood —
have a special name."

Definition card appears: "MANIFOLD — A shape where every small neighborhood
can be mapped flat without tearing."

Beat 1.3 — Three-Shape Test (2.5 min, VERIFICATION)
-----------------------------------------------------
Three shapes appear simultaneously:

1. PRETZEL (genus 2) — clearly has holes, surface looks smooth everywhere
2. PINCHED SPHERE (two spheres meeting at a point) — has a non-manifold pinch
3. CUBE — looks sharp but IS a manifold (topology != geometry)

Challenge: "Which of these are manifolds? Click to test each one."

THE CUBE TRICK QUESTION:
This is deliberately included to teach that topology != geometry. The cube's
corners LOOK sharp, but zooming in reveals that the neighborhood of a corner
is homeomorphic to a flat disc. The sharpness is a geometric property, not a
topological one. The cube IS a manifold (homeomorphic to a sphere).

Manager tests:
- Pretzel: zoom in anywhere -> flat ✓ (manifold)
- Pinched sphere: zoom in at pinch point -> two planes meeting ✗ (not manifold)
- Cube: zoom in at corner -> surprisingly, it looks flat ✓ (manifold!)

The cube surprise teaches: "Sharp corners are about GEOMETRY. Manifolds are
about TOPOLOGY — the deeper structure."

9. ACT 2: CHARTS & ATLASES (7.5 min)
================================================================================

Beat 2.1 — Peel-and-Show: What IS a Chart? (1 min)
-----------------------------------------------------
Before showing the failure, show a SUCCESS first so the manager knows what a
chart looks like.

A sphere appears. A highlighted patch (one hemisphere) peels off and flattens
into a 2D map beside the sphere. This is animated, not interactive. The flat
map has a coordinate grid.

Narration: "A chart is a flat map of part of a curved surface — like one page
in a hiking atlas."

Beat 2.2 — The One-Chart Failure (3 min)
------------------------------------------
The flat chart re-attaches to the sphere. The manager is challenged:

Challenge card: "Can one flat map cover the ENTIRE sphere? Drag to stretch the
map and find out."

INTERACTION — Click-and-Drag Chart Expansion:
- Manager drags on the chart, expanding its coverage of the sphere
- Coverage expands following a logarithmic curve:
  coverage = 0.92 * (1 - e^(-drag/200))
- Visual: the chart stretches over the sphere. Near the edge of coverage,
  grid cells become extremely distorted (long, thin)
- Color shifts from green (#34d399) at center to pink (#f472b6) at edges
  as distortion increases

THE DRAMATIC FAILURE AT 92%:
1. Chart expands smoothly to ~85%. Everything feels good.
2. Manager keeps dragging. 88%... 90%... 91%...
3. At 92%, chart edges begin to VIBRATE — a physical tremor, like something
   under strain. Grid cells are absurdly stretched (50:1 aspect ratio).
4. The chart STOPS. The uncovered region PULSES brightly.
5. Message: "No matter how far you stretch this map, there's always one point
   it can't reach. One single point — but that's enough."

CRITICAL NARRATION CONSTRAINT (Math Agent):
Must NOT say "this map covers 92%." The 92% is internal (visual asymptotic cap).
Mathematically, stereographic projection covers ALL of S² minus exactly one
point (measure zero = 100% in measure-theoretic sense). The visual limitation
is an artifact, not the mathematics.

Correct narration: "No matter how far you stretch, there's always one point it
can't reach. One single point — but that's enough. To cover the whole sphere,
you need a second map."

Beat 2.3 — The Second Chart (1.5 min)
---------------------------------------
INTERACTION — Spotlight-and-Lock:
- After the first chart fails, a second chart type appears
- Manager positions it to cover the remaining gap
- The two charts together cover the entire sphere
- Overlap region highlighted in blended color (#86efac, light green)
- A "city dot" marker in the overlap zone appears on BOTH charts at
  corresponding positions — visual proof of consistency

One-sentence verbal beat with 0.5-second red flash:
"If these maps disagreed here, you couldn't trust either one."
(The overlap region flashes red for 0.5s, then snaps back to green.)

Beat 2.4 — Naming: Chart and Atlas (30 sec)
---------------------------------------------
Definition cards appear:
- CHART: "A flat map of a piece of a curved surface — like one page in a
  hiking atlas"
- ATLAS: "A complete set of charts that covers every point, with charts
  agreeing where they overlap"

Beat 2.5 — Torus Chart Placement (VERIFICATION, 1.5 min)
----------------------------------------------------------
The sphere fades. A torus appears.

Challenge: "This shape also needs an atlas. Place charts to cover the whole
torus — leave no gaps."

The manager places 3-4 chart patches on the torus surface using spotlight-and-
lock interaction. Coverage meter tracks progress. When full coverage is achieved:
"You've built an atlas of the torus."

11. ACT 3: HOMEOMORPHISM (8.5 min)
================================================================================

Beat 3.1 — Hole Counting (2 min)
----------------------------------
Three shapes appear simultaneously:
- Sphere (0 holes)
- Torus (1 hole)
- Coffee cup (1 hole)

Challenge: "Which two of these three are 'the same shape' in disguise?"

The manager predicts. (Most will notice: torus and coffee cup both have one hole.)

THE MORPH: After the prediction, the morph slider activates. Torus smoothly
morphs into coffee cup. No tearing, no cutting. "You were right. The number of
holes is what matters."

Property Tracker: "Holes: 1" badge stays STATIC throughout the entire morph.
CRITICAL: The tracker must NOT flicker or show a different number during the
morph. The intermediate frames are vertex interpolation, not topological
operations. The tracker shows a STATIC label emphasizing "it never changed."

Beat 3.2 — Bidirectional Morph Failure (2 min)
------------------------------------------------
Now show what DOESN'T work:

SPHERE -> TORUS morph attempt:
The morph slider moves. The sphere starts to deform. At a critical point, a
DIMPLE appears — a depression trying to form the hole. But the surface would
have to tear to create the hole. The morph FREEZES. The attempted tear zone
glows pink. Message: "Can't do it. You'd have to punch a hole — that's tearing."

TORUS -> SPHERE morph attempt:
The torus starts to deform. The hole tries to close. At a critical point, a
PINCH forms — opposing walls of the hole try to merge. The morph freezes.
The pinch zone glows pink. Message: "Can't close the hole without gluing
surfaces together."

Property Tracker during failure: "Holes: 1 -> Holes: 0" with the arrow flashing
pink and a "✗" — the hole count WOULD have to change, and that's forbidden.

Beat 3.3 — Definition & Naming (30 sec)
-----------------------------------------
Definition card: HOMEOMORPHISM — "Reshape without tearing or gluing — same
holes, same structure."

Narration: "When you can reshape one into the other, mathematicians call them
homeomorphic — topologically the same."

Beat 3.4 — Three-Shape Sorting (VERIFICATION, 2 min)
------------------------------------------------------
Five shapes appear:
- Sphere (0 holes)
- Egg (0 holes)
- Torus (1 hole)
- Coffee cup (1 hole)
- Double torus / pretzel (2 holes)

The manager drags 2D thumbnail cards into labeled zones ("0 holes," "1 hole,"
"2 holes"). The 3D shapes above animate to match the grouping.

Verification: Can the manager correctly sort shapes by topological equivalence?

Beat 3.5 — Morph Slider Free Play (2 min)
-------------------------------------------
The morph slider is available for the manager to scrub freely between:
- Torus <-> Coffee cup (smooth, successful)
- Sphere <-> Egg <-> Cube (smooth, successful — cube IS homeomorphic to sphere!)
- Sphere <-> Torus (fails with dimple/pinch)

The manager can pause at any intermediate state. The Property Tracker persists:
holes stay constant for successful morphs, flash with failure for unsuccessful
ones.

13. ACT 4a: BUILDING SURFACES FROM RECTANGLES (6 min)
================================================================================

Beat 4a.1 — Torus Construction (3 min)
----------------------------------------
DUAL-VIEW LAYOUT:
- Left (60%): 3D view — the rectangle rolls up, then closes
- Right (40%): 2D "game screen" view — flat rectangle with a movable dot

A flat rectangle appears. Matching edges are highlighted:
- Left and right edges: amber (#fbbf24) with same-direction arrows
- Top and bottom edges: cyan (#22d3ee) with same-direction arrows

INTERACTION — Two-Click Construction:
1. "Glue these edges" button for left<->right. Manager clicks. The rectangle
   animates into a cylinder over 1.5 seconds. The manager WATCHES — the
   animation IS the teaching.
2. "Glue these edges" button for top<->bottom. Manager clicks. The cylinder
   bends and closes into a torus over 2 seconds.

WHY TWO-CLICK (not free-drag):
The CONCEPT is "identifying edges." The concept is NOT "3D spatial
manipulation." By automating the physical deformation and letting the manager
choose WHAT to glue, cognitive load stays on the math, not the interface.

DUAL VIEW — Game Screen:
After the torus is built, the manager can click "Play" on the right panel.
A glowing dot appears on the flat rectangle. It can be moved:
- Exit right edge -> enter left edge (wrapping)
- Exit top -> enter bottom (wrapping)
Simultaneously, the corresponding dot moves on the 3D torus in the left panel.

This IS the Pac-Man/Asteroids connection: the flat game screen IS a chart of
the torus. The edge-wrapping IS the global topology.

After 45 seconds, the game screen minimizes to a small inset (to prevent it
from becoming a toy that distracts from the lesson flow).

Slider Snap at t=1:
The "Glue" animation uses a slider internally. The snap at t=1 (when the edge
identification is complete) signals the topological event — the moment the
surface changes from "rectangle" to "cylinder" or from "cylinder" to "torus."

Beat 4a.2 — Klein Bottle Construction (3 min)
-----------------------------------------------
Same starting rectangle. Same left<->right edges (amber, same direction).
BUT: top and bottom edges have REVERSED arrows (cyan, arrows pointing in
opposite directions).

Step 1: "Glue matching edges" for left<->right. Same cylinder as before.
Step 2: The remaining edges have REVERSED arrows. Before the manager clicks:

MOBIUS STRIP MICRO-MOMENT (15 seconds):
A small annotation appears near the reversed arrows: "These arrows are reversed
— a twist." Alongside, a tiny inline animation (120x120px) shows a Mobius strip
forming from a twisted strip. 3 seconds. A line traces along it and returns
reversed.
Text: "A twist creates a one-sided surface. Let's see what this twist does to
our rectangle."

The manager clicks "Glue these edges." The cylinder deforms. The surface MUST
pass through itself. Self-intersection highlighted with pulsing pink (#f472b6).

NARRATION AT SELF-INTERSECTION:
"See where the tube passes through itself? That's not really happening — it's
like a shadow that overlaps itself. In four dimensions, there's room for the
tube to go around without touching. We just can't show you 4D on a screen."

KLEIN BOTTLE GAME SCREEN:
Same dual-view as torus. But when the dot exits the top-right, it enters the
bottom-LEFT (horizontal mirror). The mirror-flip IS non-orientability, made
visceral through the game mechanic.

Klein bottle slider doesn't stop at self-intersection — the self-intersection
is representational, not topological.

14. ACT 4b: NON-ORIENTABILITY (6.5 min)
================================================================================

Beat 4b.1 — The Traced-Line Non-Orientability Test (2.5 min)
--------------------------------------------------------------
TORUS (left): An arrow is placed on the surface. It traces a path that goes
"around" the torus (through the hole, around the body). When it returns to its
starting point, the arrow points the SAME direction. Orientation preserved.

KLEIN BOTTLE (right): Same test. An arrow traces a path along the Klein bottle
surface. It follows a specific pre-computed loop that traverses the orientation-
reversing cycle. When it returns to its starting point, the arrow points the
OPPOSITE direction. Orientation reversed.

The two tests play side by side for comparison.

NARRATION:
"On the torus, a clockwise arrow stays clockwise — it has a consistent
'inside' and 'outside.' On the Klein bottle, the arrow comes back flipped.
There IS no consistent inside or outside."

This is the honest test of non-orientability: orientation reversal along a
closed loop.

WHY TRACED-LINE (not paint test as primary):
The paint test was debated extensively. The Simplifier Agent argued for paint
(more intuitive), while the Math Agent argued for the traced-line/arrow-flip
(more mathematically precise). The resolution: traced-line is PRIMARY (it IS
the definition of non-orientability), paint is SECONDARY (a confirmation that
makes it visceral).

Beat 4b.2 — Paint Confirmation (1.5 min)
------------------------------------------
Torus: Manager clicks a point. Blue paint floods one side (outside). The inside
remains unpainted. "Two sides."

Klein bottle: Manager clicks a point. Blue paint starts spreading... and
eventually covers the ENTIRE surface. "One side."

This confirms what the arrow test showed, in a more intuitive visual format.

Beat 4b.3 — Definition & Naming (30 sec)
------------------------------------------
Definition card: NON-ORIENTABLE — "A surface where a clockwise loop can come
back counterclockwise."

Beat 4b.4 — Three-Shape Classification Test (VERIFICATION, 2 min)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Three shapes appear. The manager must identify which surface "has no inside":
- Sphere (orientable, 0 holes)
- Torus (orientable, 1 hole)
- Klein bottle (non-orientable, 1 hole)

Key insight being tested: The torus and Klein bottle have the SAME number of
holes, but are topologically DIFFERENT because of orientability. Holes alone
don't tell the full story. This is the lesson's deepest insight.

15. THE BRIDGE: DATA HAS SHAPE (2.5 min)
================================================================================

THE HOUSE PRICE SURFACE:
A 3D surface appears showing home prices as a function of square footage and
school quality rating.

Axes:
- X: "Square footage" (500-3000 sqft)
- Y: "School rating" (1-10)
- Z (height): "Home price" ($200K-$800K)

Surface generated from a Gaussian sum:
  f(u,v) = Sum_i A_i * exp(-((u-mu_i^x)^2 + (v-mu_i^y)^2) / (2*sigma_i^2))

This creates a landscape of "hills" — high-price neighborhoods.

RENDERING:
- Mesh: 60x60 grid (7,200 triangles)
- Surface: gradient from #1e3a5f (valleys) through #38bdf8 (mid) to #fbbf24
  (amber peaks), mapped to height, custom ShaderMaterial with smoothstep ramps
- Wireframe overlay: thin white lines at 8% opacity ("data grid" feel)
- Scattered data points: ~200 small pink spheres (radius 0.02) hovering just
  above/below the surface (showing the surface is a FIT, not the data itself)
- Orbit controls enabled (drag to rotate), no zoom, no pan
- Auto-rotate at 0.3 rad/s, stops when manager touches it

THE TEACHING BEAT (narration-synced):
1. Surface appears, auto-rotating.
   "This surface shows home prices based on size and school quality."
2. Camera glides to a close-up on one peak. A small flat tangent plane appears
   (same green from the zoom test).
   "Zoom into any neighborhood on this surface — it looks flat. It's a manifold."
3. Camera pulls back.
   "And just like the sphere, you'd need charts to map it flat."
4. Beat.
   "Your company's data has shapes like this. The math you just learned —
    charts, atlases, homeomorphism — that's how we navigate them."

CRITICAL CONSTRAINT (Math Agent):
The narration must NOT say "all data lives on manifolds." The honest framing:
"When data has structure, it often lies on or near a manifold. The tools you
just learned — charts, atlases, neighborhoods — are exactly how mathematicians
and ML engineers study that structure."
The word "often" is doing real work.

WHAT IS NOT INCLUDED (to keep scope tight):
- No clickable zoom on the House Price Surface (callback is narration-only)
- No chart-placing interaction (already taught on the sphere)
- No data-point hovering or tooltips (this is a bridge, not a new lesson)

================================================================================
16. THE FIVE DEFINITION CARDS
================================================================================

All five cards passed mathematical audit by the Math Agent (Round 9):

1. MANIFOLD
   "A shape where every small neighborhood can be mapped flat without tearing."
   Math: "Mapped flat" = homeomorphic to R^n. "Without tearing" = continuous.

2. CHART
   "A flat map of a piece of a curved surface — like one page in a hiking atlas."
   Math: "Piece" = open subset. "Flat map" = image in R^n. Note: "small" was
   deliberately dropped — charts can be large.

3. ATLAS
   "A complete set of charts that covers every point, with charts agreeing
   where they overlap."
   Math: "Agreeing" = transition functions continuous. "Every point" = covering.

4. HOMEOMORPHISM
   "Reshape without tearing or gluing — same holes, same structure."
   Math: "Reshape" = continuous bijection. "Without tearing" = continuous.
   "Without gluing" = injective with continuous inverse. "Same holes" = genus
   preserved.

5. NON-ORIENTABLE
   "A surface where a clockwise loop can come back counterclockwise."

18. TWO-PATH FRAMING (ESSENTIAL vs COMPLETE)
================================================================================

The lesson landing page presents two paths:

ESSENTIAL (~35 min): Acts 1-3 + Bridge
  "Understand manifolds, charts, and homeomorphism"

COMPLETE (~55 min): All acts
  "Plus: build surfaces and explore non-orientability"

Both paths are valid. Neither feels like "the short version."

RATIONALE (Pedagogy Agent, Round 10):
- Most corporate learning sessions are 45 minutes or less
- Fatigue drop-off around minute 35-40, right when Act 4 (the most
  conceptually demanding material) begins
- The "I'll finish later" problem — managers pause and never return
- The Essential path delivers genuine understanding of the three core
  concepts (manifold, chart/atlas, homeomorphism)
- Act 4 is an explicit "Go Deeper" option, not a truncation

A manager completing the Essential path can explain to a colleague:
- "A manifold is a shape where every small piece can be mapped flat."
- "A chart is one of those flat maps. An atlas is the complete set."
- "Two shapes are homeomorphic if they have the same number of holes."

That's genuine understanding. That's the foundation for the ML lesson.

19. INTERACTION DESIGN DECISIONS
================================================================================

SMART ZOOM (Act 1):
- Camera dollies to 1.5 units from clicked surface point (500ms)
- Small context indicator (80x80px, top-left) shows full shape with glowing dot
- Click-to-click: camera pans directly without zooming out (400ms)
- No full-screen zoom/unzoom cycles (avoids 8+ sluggish transitions)

CHART EXPANSION (Act 2):
- Click-and-drag for chart expansion (failure discovery)
- Logarithmic coverage: coverage = 0.92 * (1 - e^(-drag/200))
- At 92%, chart edges vibrate (physical tremor under strain)
- Spotlight-and-lock for chart placement (success/construction)

TWO-CLICK CONSTRUCTION (Act 4):
- Automated animations for edge-gluing (the manager chooses WHAT, not HOW)
- Cognitive load stays on the concept ("identifying edges"), not the
  interface ("how do I drag this 3D thing")

DUAL-VIEW GAME SCREEN (Act 4):
- Left 60%: 3D construction view
- Right 40%: 2D flat rectangle with movable dot
- Dot wraps at edges on the 2D view; corresponding dot moves on 3D shape
- Game screen minimizes to inset after 45 seconds to prevent toy distraction

MORPH SLIDER (Act 3):
- Horizontal slider, scrubbable to any position
- Manager can park at 40% and stare at intermediate state
- Smooth vertex interpolation with same mesh topology throughout
- Slider must be physically large enough to feel explorative, not just
  a demo trigger

PROPERTY TRACKER (Holes counter):
- Small persistent badge: "Holes: N" with checkmark
- Appears during morphs, stays STATIC (no flickering, no intermediate values)
- Holes-only version (not the original multi-property dashboard)
- Only tracks genus, which actually CHANGES between shapes we show
  (sphere=0, torus=1, double torus=2)
- Connected components always 1, boundary components always 0 — tracking
  numbers that never change teaches nothing

GALLERY WALL REPLAY CLIPS:
- Option A (selected): 3-second LOOPING animations in the background canvas
- Pre-scripted camera paths showing the key moment
- The replay is a REMINDER, not a re-experience
- Example: chart stretching to 92% and snapping back as a 3-second loop

21. KEY DEBATES & RESOLUTIONS
================================================================================

1. CUBE AS NON-MANIFOLD EXAMPLE
   DEBATE: Pedagogy Agent initially proposed cube corners as "not manifold."
   RESOLUTION: Math Agent corrected this — a cube IS a manifold (homeomorphic
   to sphere). Corners are geometric, not topological. The cube became a TRICK
   QUESTION in the Three-Shape Test, teaching topology != geometry.

2. LIDDED vs OPEN COFFEE CUP
   DEBATE: Coffee cup morph to torus — is the cup open or closed?
   RESOLUTION: Math Agent: an open coffee cup has boundary and is NOT
   homeomorphic to a torus. The cup must be "lidded" (closed surface, genus 1).

3. ARROW-FLIP vs PAINT for NON-ORIENTABILITY
   DEBATE: Simplifier wanted paint (intuitive), Math Agent wanted arrow-flip
   (precise). Pedagogy Agent wanted both.
   RESOLUTION: Traced-line/arrow-flip is PRIMARY (it IS the definition). Paint
   test is SECONDARY confirmation. 15-second Mobius strip reference integrated
   into Klein bottle construction as a micro-moment, not a full beat.

4. BRIDGE LENGTH (2.5 min vs 5 min)
   DEBATE: Simplifier wanted 5 minutes to fully explain data manifolds.
   Pedagogy Agent argued 2.5 minutes.
   RESOLUTION: 2.5 minutes. At minute 44, attention is draining. The bridge is
   a teaser/seed, not a second lesson. Math Agent backed this: the bridge makes
   ONE honest claim ("data can also live on manifolds") and that's enough.

5. PROPERTY TRACKER SCOPE
   DEBATE: Original proposal had multi-property dashboard (holes, orientability,
   surface area, curvature). Simplifier wanted it cut entirely.
   RESOLUTION: Holes-only version. Just "Holes: 1 ✓". It's the one invariant
   that actually CHANGES between shapes we show. Connected components (always 1)
   and boundary components (always 0) teach nothing. The reduction from
   dashboard to single number is more honest, not less.

6. COLD OPEN: INTERACTIVE vs SPECTACLE
   DEBATE: Simplifier wanted interactive "poke the sphere" at second zero.
   Pedagogy Agent wanted looping morph.
   RESOLUTION: Looping morph (spectacle). At second zero, the manager hasn't
   agreed to participate. An interactive prompt risks "why am I doing this?"
   The morph earns attention. Interactivity comes 30 seconds later.

7. "BAD OVERLAP" FAILURE CASE
   DEBATE: Simplifier proposed showing charts that disagree in their overlap
   zone (a pedagogical failure case).
   RESOLUTION: Rejected. Math Agent: "bad overlaps" don't exist in manifold
   theory — if transition functions aren't continuous, you don't have an atlas.
   Showing a "bad overlap" would require explaining transition functions, which
   opens a pedagogical tar pit. The one-chart failure (92%) already provides
   the necessary "why you need more" moment.

8. VIDEO GAME ANALOGY PROMINENCE
   DEBATE: Math Agent proposed Pac-Man/Asteroids as major framing for torus &
   Klein bottle. Pedagogy Agent wanted it reduced to one sentence.
   RESOLUTION: The dual-view game screen (2D rectangle + 3D shape) makes the
   video game analogy VISUAL and AUTOMATIC without verbal explanation. The flat
   rectangle IS the game screen. The wrapping IS the game mechanic. Shown, not
   told.

9. MATH AGENT'S "COORDINATE CRISIS" REFRAMING
   DEBATE: Math Agent proposed starting with "when do coordinates break?" instead
   of "locally flat."
   RESOLUTION: Rejected for primary framing (too abstract for managers), but
   elements woven in: charts in Act 2 are framed as solving a COORDINATE problem
   ("you can't put consistent coordinates on the whole sphere"), and the bridge
   uses coordinate language for ML connection.

10. 92% NARRATION PRECISION
    DEBATE: Should the narration mention a specific percentage?
    RESOLUTION: No. The 92% is internal (visual asymptotic cap). Mathematically,
    stereographic projection covers S² minus exactly one point (measure zero).
    Narration says "there's always one point it can't reach" — not a percentage.

11. CLASSIFICATION THEOREM AS CLOSING MOMENT
    DEBATE: Math Agent proposed ending with the classification theorem ("two
    numbers classify all closed surfaces").
    RESOLUTION: Not included as a formal teaching moment, but implicitly present
    in Act 4b's final test (which reveals that holes + orientability together
    classify the shapes we've shown).

22. MATHEMATICAL VALIDATION NOTES
================================================================================

The Math Agent performed a final mathematical audit (Rounds 8-11):

CONCEPTS VALIDATED AS CORRECT:
- Every definition card passes mathematical review (Section 16)
- Figure-eight crossing: excellent non-manifold example (removing crossing
  point gives 4 components; removing any line point gives at most 2)
- Pinched sphere: correct non-manifold (removing pinch gives 2 components;
  removing any disc point gives 1)
- Cube IS a manifold — neighborhood of corner is homeomorphic to open disc
- Sphere requires minimum 2 charts (stereographic projection covers S^2
  minus one point)
- Torus requires minimum 2 charts (but 3-4 shown for visual clarity)
- Genus is complete invariant for closed orientable surfaces (classification
  theorem)
- Klein bottle self-intersection is an artifact of R^3 immersion, not a
  property of the surface
- House Price Surface with Gaussian sum is a smooth 2-manifold (diffeomorphic
  to R^2)

KEY MATHEMATICAL ERRORS CAUGHT AND CORRECTED:
1. Cube corners are NOT non-manifold (topology != geometry)
2. Coffee cup must be closed/lidded (open cup has boundary)
3. Paper-wrapping conflates metric rigidity with topological obstruction
   (replaced with stretchy sheet)
4. Hole-counting restricted to orientable surfaces only
5. 92% coverage is a visual cap, not the mathematics
6. "All data lives on manifolds" is false — "often" is required

MORPH INTERPOLATION CAVEAT:
The morph between torus and coffee cup is purely visual (vertex lerp), NOT
topological. Intermediate frames are NOT homeomorphisms. This is fine as long
as we never claim "every frame is a valid manifold." The lesson correctly
presents the morph as "these two shapes are equivalent" without claiming the
animation itself is a mathematical operation.

KLEIN BOTTLE SELF-INTERSECTION NARRATION:
Must be framed as: "The self-intersection is an artifact of 3D rendering.
The Klein bottle 'wants' to live in 4D. We're seeing its shadow in 3D, like
how a 3D object's shadow on a wall can overlap even though the object doesn't."
